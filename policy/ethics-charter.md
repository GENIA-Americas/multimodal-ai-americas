# Ethics Charter
## AI Corridor of the Americas

GENIA Americas Corporation
Version 1.0

---

## Preamble

This charter defines the ethical commitments binding on all
organizations participating in the AI Corridor of the Americas.
It applies to the Glapagos platform, the RaceFor.AI network, and
all research and deployment activities coordinated under GENIA Americas.

Alignment is with WHO and OECD AI principles. Where regional context
requires more specific commitments, this charter is more specific.

Compliance with this charter is a condition of participation in the
Corridor, not a voluntary aspiration.

---

## Commitments

### 1. Transparency

All AI systems deployed through the Corridor must be auditable.

- Decision logic must be documented at a level sufficient for an
  independent reviewer to understand how outputs are produced.
- Training data sources must be disclosed, including provenance,
  collection methodology, and known limitations.
- Model performance must be reported against regional benchmarks,
  not only global ones.
- Significant model updates must be documented and communicated to
  affected stakeholders.

### 2. Accountability

Accountability for AI outcomes is not distributed to the point of
disappearing.

- Every AI system deployed through the Corridor must have a named
  accountable party at the organizational level.
- Escalation pathways for AI-related harms must be defined before
  deployment, not after an incident.
- Network-level accountability sits with GENIA Americas Corporation
  for systems it directly operates, and with the deploying organization
  for systems it operates under the Corridor license.

### 3. Inclusion and Representation

The Americas contains hundreds of languages, dozens of legal traditions,
and enormous demographic diversity. AI systems that ignore this are
not inclusive — they are exclusionary by design.

- Training data must reflect the demographic, linguistic, and economic
  range of the intended deployment context. Homogeneous datasets are
  not acceptable for heterogeneous populations.
- Regional languages — including Spanish, Portuguese, indigenous
  languages, and English — must be supported where they are spoken
  by the population being served.
- Communities affected by AI systems have the right to participate
  in the design and evaluation process. This is not a consultation
  formality; it is a design requirement.
- Systems must be evaluated for differential performance across
  demographic groups before production deployment.

### 4. Privacy and Consent

- Data collected from individuals must be collected with informed
  consent, stored with appropriate security, and used only for
  purposes disclosed at collection.
- Data protection standards must meet or exceed the applicable
  legal requirements in each jurisdiction where data originates.
- Federated and privacy-preserving computation approaches are
  preferred when they achieve comparable model quality.
- Data subjects retain rights over their data as specified by
  applicable law. Systems must be designed to honor these rights
  operationally, not just in policy documents.

### 5. Security

- Cybersecurity risk assessment is required before production
  deployment of any AI system in the Corridor.
- Adversarial robustness must be evaluated for systems operating
  in high-stakes domains: public health, financial services, public
  safety, and government services.
- Incident response procedures for AI-specific failure modes —
  model degradation, data poisoning, adversarial inputs — must be
  defined and tested.
- Security architecture must be reviewed when the deployment context
  changes materially.

### 6. Non-Subordination

The AI Corridor of the Americas is not an extension of any external
actor's AI strategy.

- Partnerships with global platforms and technology providers are
  pursued on terms that preserve the hemisphere's ability to make
  independent decisions about its AI infrastructure.
- Standards adopted by the Corridor are evaluated on technical merit
  and regional fit, not on the basis of which external actor promotes
  them.
- Participating organizations are not required to use infrastructure
  controlled by external actors where hemisphere-native alternatives
  exist at comparable capability.

## Enforcement

Charter compliance is reviewed through:

- Annual self-assessment by participating organizations
- Peer review within working groups
- Incident review process for reported violations
- Network Council deliberation for systemic non-compliance

Non-compliance is handled through a graduated process: documented
remediation plan, public notice if remediation fails, suspension of
Corridor membership for persistent violation.

## Revision

This charter is a living document. Proposed revisions are submitted

The Network Council ratifies changes by consensus.
